The Runaway Bride
Season 2 · Chapter 4
✨ TWIST
I left the altar in my socked feet and found my actual life
S2 · Ch 4
~4 min read
✍️ Ifeanyi_Story

The morning of my wedding I woke up at four a.m. and lay in the dark listening to the hotel air conditioning hum. My mother was asleep two rooms down. The dress was hanging near the window like a white ghost.

I did not feel afraid. That was the strange thing. I felt something closer to clarity — the kind that arrives only when a long argument finally ends, not in victory, but in exhaustion.

✦

Four hundred guests. The ceremony was in the hotel ballroom — high ceilings, imported flowers, a string quartet that my mother-in-law had insisted upon. My dress had cost four hundred and twenty thousand naira. I had worn it to one fitting and cried, which everyone assumed was joy.

I removed my heels during the vows. Not dramatically. I simply bent and set them neatly beside my feet while the pastor was still reading. My maid of honour, Sade, noticed and looked at me with a question I did not answer.

The pastor reached the part everyone knows.

"Speak now, or forever hold your peace."

My mother was smiling so hard her eyes had nearly closed. My father was watching me with the look he used to give me as a child when he was trying to tell me something without words. I looked at Emeka — my soon-to-be husband, my six-year relationship, my plan — and I thought:

I know about the other woman. I have known for eleven months. I have been waiting for it to stop mattering. It never stopped mattering.

✦

Four hundred people heard what I said next. The exact words do not matter now. What matters is that I said them clearly, without crying, and without looking away from his face.

His expression did something I had not expected. It did not collapse into guilt. It collapsed into relief. As if he had also been waiting for eleven months for someone to say it out loud.

In my socked feet, I walked down the aisle.

My father stood up as I passed him. I shook my head, just slightly. He sat back down. I loved him enormously in that moment.

Outside the ballroom there was a corridor with blue carpet and a fire exit. I pushed through the fire exit into the service stairwell and sat on the third step and called my cousin Adaeze, who lived nearby.

"Come now," I said. "Don't ask anything. Just come."

✦

The video was viewed four million times in eleven days. A stranger filmed it — one of the string quartet, I later learned, who had placed a phone on a music stand. I do not blame him. I would have filmed it too.

I did not watch it for three weeks. When I finally did, what struck me most was how quiet I was. How deliberate. You could see the heels placed neatly on the floor. You could see my father standing. You could see the exact moment I stopped being afraid of the wrong things.

In the second week I started baking. I had no reason to — I had never baked seriously before. But my hands needed something to do and flour was what was in the kitchen.

By the third week, three strangers had contacted me through the video asking if I could make what I had posted on my phone. I had posted a photograph of a cardamom loaf. I had not thought of it as a product.

Six months later I opened Bare Kitchen.

Bare — because I had stood in front of four hundred people with nothing hidden. Because the name felt honest in a way I was trying to become.

The morning we opened, a woman came in who I did not recognise. She ordered the cardamom loaf and sat at the small table near the window for two hours. When she was leaving she stopped at the counter.

"I walked out of a job I had hated for seven years after I watched your video," she said. "I wanted you to know that."

I thanked her. After she left I went to the back and stood quietly for a moment before the next order came in.

I have thought about that woman often. About the small, unintended permission that one person's honesty can give another. About how the things we do in our most frightened, most certain moments ripple outward in ways we will never fully know.

I still do not wear heels inside the bakery.
